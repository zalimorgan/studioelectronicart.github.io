Reflection on this semesters work in relation to the internets materiality
15 May 2020

In week 1 we were asked to address the topic of truth in the form of a vlog. I chose to explore introspective honesty and the pursuit of subjective truth via the fortune telling practices of Astrology and Tarot. These practices rely on the perceived authenticity of tradition and ritual to deliver universal statements that can be read in any number of ways that suit the reader. Astrology and Tarot are popular topics online, many social media accounts (serious or not) are built around them. In the past, a role performed by an expert in star charts and people reading, it’s now a simple online survey that generates your zodiac, ascendant sign’s etcetera. The removal of the human practitioner from this experience obscures the previously obvious value extraction of currency exchange for expert-pseudo-psych validation, and replaces it with advertisements, and data collection in exchange for pages of personality traits, predictions and sketchy illustrations of anthropomorphised animals in cosmic habitats.


In addition to online streaming content like Assembly Line Yeah! the production and format of my vlog’s were influenced by the short film Heaven-Hell by experimental film maker Dirk de Bruyn. de Bruyn collage’s together footage and audio to frame a gross image of limbo between the future and the past. The image is formatted like a particularly intense webpage, blocked into headers, navigation etc with scrolling objects interrupting confronting bodily footage (all collected from the internet archive) demanding your attention like permissions and advertisements. I read the random juxtaposition of emoji-like icons bouncing across the frame as critique on global communication technology’s irreverent constant demand for our attention. I also felt that it acknowledged the common practice of viewing multiple screens simultaneously and the viewers growing appetite for information saturation. I wanted my vlogs to embrace this, and documented myself from multiple angles to deliver differently paced streams of information. I further fragmented the documentation of my own image to obscure my physical presence in the video and ultimately online.

In the following weeks we engaged with Kaprow’s “The Education of the Un-Artist” (1993, 97) a text concerned with the complex delineation distinguishing Art from things that are not Art. In reflection on the internet’s status as a site, support, and material interest of countless artists it fitsfairly neatly into Kaprow’s delineation of Nonart. This is extended as Boris Groys describes in the introducing paragraphs of “Art Workers: between Utopia and the Archive” (2013), that the internet has been merged into the institutional hegemony of the Art-world, just like Kaprow’s Nonart before it. The blog discussions around the importance of intent and authorship in digital artworks leads me to engage with the internet as a ’Nonart’ material and support that is active in my practice. I am challenged by this as I find the internet to be both a negative and positive experience. In the past I have faced ethical challenges around the use and waste of unsustainable materials, for which I have developed processes to minimize new materials and techniques to avoid waste. My dilemma with the internet is one of principals and a little fear, to avoid or minimize will perpetuate a surface level engagement in concept and outcome.


The blog discussions around the importance of intent and authorship in digital artworks leads me to engage with the internet as a ’Nonart’ material and support that is active in my practice. I am challenged by this as I find the internet to be both a negative and positive experience. In the past I have faced ethical challenges around the use and waste of unsustainable materials, for which I have developed processes to minimize new materials and techniques to avoid waste. My dilemma with the internet is one of principals and a little fear, to avoid or minimize will perpetuate a surface level engagement in concept and outcome. As an incomplete work I have not fully grappled with the implications of my intentions, however I feel that it represents progressin my understanding of the internet’s material place in my artistic practice. I have moved from the production of a vlog on a HTML website mostly disconnected from the majority of the internet, to the employment of the internet’s structures as a tool for documentation, exhibition and in the near future potential acceptance of my subjective image drowning amongst the cacophony of voices.
